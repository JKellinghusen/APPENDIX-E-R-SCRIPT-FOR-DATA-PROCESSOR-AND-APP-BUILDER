Build a culture of gratitude and recognition

Why it matters

Workplaces that practice gratitude and recognition foster employee engagement, motivation, and retention. For aid organizations, where employees often work in high-pressure environments, recognition plays a critical role in maintaining well-being and resilience. A culture of appreciation can improve team cohesion, prevent burnout, and enhance overall effectiveness.

Moreover, fostering a culture of gratitude creates a ripple effect, as employees who feel appreciated are more likely to recognize and support their peers, contributing to a positive and collaborative workplace environment.

Recommendations

Establish formal recognition programs

Whether it’s the tried-and-tested “Employee of the Month” or a more innovative model, structured recognition programs ensure that employees at all levels receive meaningful recognition. Follow best practices when implementing these programs, such as ensuring that recognition is connected to your mission as well as specific, timely, and provides meaningful rewards. Rewards can be equally creative, and while cash is a popular option, alternatives include additional paid time off, branded goods, and professional development opportunities. Creative rewards tailored to employees’ interests are a great way to demonstrate appreciation.

Ensure that someone – perhaps even a whole team – is dedicated to managing staff recognition. These programs can be a big lift and if they are inconsistent, poorly executed, or lackluster, they can lose their effectiveness or even backfire – if they are perceived as placating rather than genuine.

Build a culture of support and recognition

While recognition from leaders can be very meaningful, a culture of gratitude and recognition that permeates the organization empowers employees to appreciate one another and build a more positive workplace. Leaders and managers can foster peer-to-peer recognition by celebrating employee contributions through verbal praise, written appreciation, or public acknowledgments, and motivating their staff to do the same.

Encourage managers to adapt their approach to their team and work environment. A field office may have a physical “appreciation wall,” while a remote team can incorporate shoutouts into their standing meetings.
