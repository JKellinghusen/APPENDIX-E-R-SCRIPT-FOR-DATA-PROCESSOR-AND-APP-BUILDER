Respect boundaries between work and non-work time

Why it matters

Blurred boundaries between work and personal time – feeling pressured to be available outside of working hours – contribute to psychological distress, burnout, and decreased overall well-being.

For humanitarian and development workers, the challenge of separating work from personal time is even greater. Many employees work in crisis settings, across multiple time zones, or in roles where urgent needs arise unpredictably. While some level of flexibility is necessary, constant availability can lead to burnout and hinder long-term retention. Organizations that proactively set clear boundaries between work and personal time can improve workforce sustainability, reduce stress, and increase overall effectiveness.

Recommendations

Establish clear digital boundaries

One of the most significant contributors to boundary violations is digital communication, particularly emails, messaging apps, and work calls outside of office hours. Establish clear policies on after-hours communication, such as limiting work-related messages to business hours and ensuring that employees do not feel pressured to respond outside of their designated time. Pair employees who are “on call” outside of working hours with least one counterpart to divide on call duties.

Encourage leadership to model healthy boundaries

Workplace culture is heavily influenced by leadership behavior. When managers and senior staff respect their own personal time and avoid engaging in non-essential work communication outside of office hours, employees feel empowered to do the same. Leaders can help create a healthier work environment by setting expectations that protect work-life balance.

Support flexible scheduling without overextending workers

Providing flexibility in work hours can benefit employees managing personal responsibilities, but it must be implemented thoughtfully. Give employees the ability to adjust their schedules to accommodate their needs while ensuring that flexibility does not translate into an "always-on" culture. Structured flexibility – such as core work hours with optional start and end times – helps employees maintain a better work-life balance.
