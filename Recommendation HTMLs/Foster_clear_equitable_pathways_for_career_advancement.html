Foster clear, equitable pathways for career advancement

Why it matters

Transparent career pathways provide employees with a clear understanding of the skills and experiences required for advancement, reducing ambiguity and potential biases in promotion processes and increasing employee satisfaction and retention. This clarity is especially beneficial for underrepresented groups, as it helps in identifying and addressing systemic barriers to progression. Even in the aid sector, systemic racism and colonialism prevail, requiring deliberate efforts to create equitable career advancement pathways that dismantle existing power imbalances.

 

Recommendations

Develop transparent career progression frameworks

Create and share with your staff a career progression policy that outlines the necessary skills, experiences, and competencies required for each role or tier. Utilize objective metrics wherever possible to negate potential bias. This transparency enables employees to understand what is needed for advancement and to plan their professional development accordingly.

If your organization’s staffing is relatively stable and big promotions and raises are not feasible, consider alternative methods of career advancement, such as improvements in work hours, pay, benefits, commute time, responsibility, and self-fulfillment.

Empower your staff to grow and progress

Building the ladder is not enough, you must also help your employees climb it. Ask managers to stay aware of their employees’ goals and aspirations and support their growth within the organization, incentivizing them to stay. Offer resources such as training programs, tuition reimbursement, and mentorship opportunities to help employees reach their goals.

Address systemic barriers and promote inclusion

Throughout this process, you must actively identify and dismantle systemic barriers that hinder the advancement of marginalized groups. This includes implementing policies that promote diversity, equity, and inclusion, and ensuring that remote or hybrid work arrangements do not limit access to career growth opportunities.
